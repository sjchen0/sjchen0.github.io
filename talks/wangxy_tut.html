Symmetry and Invariances in Machine Learning

Lagrangian system

Consider a dynamic system \(q(t)\), which is a curve moving in \(\mathbb R^d\) along time, governed by some update rule described as ODEs. For example, if \(q\) stands for the weights of a neural network trained by GD, the governing rule for \(q\), in the continuous-time limit, is the gradient flow \(\dot q=-\nabla F(q)\), where \(F\) is the loss function.

Another example in phsyics: \(q\) stands for the spatial coordinates of an objective moving in \(\mathbb R^d\) driven by a conservative potential energy \(U\), which exerts a force \(-\nabla U(q)\). Then Newton’s law says \(m\ddot q=-\nabla U(q)\).

A Lagrangian of a dynamic system is a real-valued function \(L(q(t),\dot q(t),t):\mathbb R^d\times\mathbb R^d\times\mathbb R\to\mathbb R\) that satisfies \[ \frac{d}{dt}\left(\frac{\partial L}{\partial \dot q}\right)=\frac{\partial L}{\partial q}, \] named Euler-Lagrange equation.

In other words, you should find a Lagrangian such that its Euler-Lagrange equation must agree with the governing rule of the system. This is nontrivial in general: some system may not have a Lagrangian at all.

Fortunately, however, we know that a system driven by a family of gradient algorithms, such as GD and momentum GD, has a Lagrangian function of the form \[ L(q,\dot q,t)=\frac{1}{2}e^{\alpha_t}\dot q^\top\dot q-e^{\beta_t}F(q). \] As expected, by taking appropriate \(\alpha_t,\beta_t\), its Euler-Lagrange equation is exactly the update rule.

Noether’s theorem

To describe symmetry, we first need a notation that moves \(q\) according to the symmetry. It is a map parameterized by a scalar \(s\): \(q\mapsto Q(q,s)\), with \(s\) charaterizing the extent of the transformation and \(Q(q,0)=q\). For example, in rotation symmetry, you want to rotate \(q\) by the map and \(s\) can be the rotation angle.

Symmetry means invariance of Lagrangian under this map. Namely, if you slightly move \(q\mapsto Q\) for infinitesimal \(s\), the corresponding Lagrangian \(L(Q(q,s),\dot Q(q,s),t)\) remains intact: \[ \frac{\partial L}{\partial s}=0. \] Noether’s theorem says that if you have such a symmetry, then you have the conservation law \[ \left<\frac{\partial L}{\partial\dot q},\frac{\partial Q}{\partial s}\right>=\text{const}. \] Specifically, when \(F\) is rotation-invariant, then \(L\) is rotation-invariant.
